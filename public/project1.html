Summary

We designed an avatar based game to empower folks against misinformation. As they progress through the game, they learn to look at content critically. We are employing multiple persuasive design strategies like fictionalization and obfuscation. We relied on embedded game design in order to avoid reactance from users which generally manifests when people have their actions and beliefs challenged. We look to build upon our understanding of goal pursuit and persuasion through communication to mitigate dissonance created by the “fake” news around us.

Project Overview

Veritas, an avatar-based game, empowers individuals and organizations to stay educated on misinformation by learning to examine content critically.

Generative Methods

We started with our literature review by focusing on instances of mishaps arising out of misinformation. We learned about the different pathways people receive misinformation and important characteristics of misinformation, especially in the form of blurbs that are “convenient”, “efficient”, and “trustworthy” sources. We also conducted research on virtual pet games and found they can serve as a catalyst for behaviour change.

Evaluative Methods

The brainstorming sessions put us on a path towards a tamagotchi-like game concept where users take care of fictional, virtual, and animated creatures. Through taking care and generating affinity for the personalized creature, we designed the game to show that misinformation can affect individuals directly, and even cause harm. Our initial user-testing results (think aloud sessions) along with our paper-prototyping helped us define the problem.

Embedded Design

Embedded design obscures a product’s persuasive intent in order to make people consider diverse viewpoints or think about sensitive issues. Obfuscation uses framing devices or genres that divert expectations away from the a product’s persuasive intent, while distancing employs fiction and metaphor to increase the psychological gap between players’ identities and beliefs and the game’s characters and persuasive content.

Background Research

Fake news can be defined as the dissemination of incorrect or misleading information. Fake news may be in the form of misinformation, which is inaccurate content not intended to deceive, or disinformation, which involves disseminating deliberately false information
Bogus news has been around a lot longer than real news. And it’s left a lot of destruction behind.

What kind of surveillance system?

Surveillance in public health has a long history, there are many specialized surveillance systems including sentinel surveillance, zoonotic disease surveillance, adverse events surveillance and more. We did some preliminary research in the types of surveillance system that were widely used such as ProMed and Health Map.

What indicators are necessary?

Certain dimensions of infectious disease events are important for different reasons. These are some dimensions an analysts would need to know to evaluate risk: incidence rates, prevalence rates, severity, mortality, etc.

Feedback from previous product

“What’s up in Infectious Disease” was a previous product that simplistically displayed a curated number of infectious disease events. We took feedback from this system as a starting point.

Co-design workshop

Since building a product like this required the collaboration of people with many different expertises, I decided that a co-design workshop should be in place. I gathered representatives from different teams within the company and ran the workshop with a UX researcher on my team.

Workshop goal: generate ideas from different perspectives and define values based on different disciplines.

Activity 1: Brainstorm and discuss

a. What questions should the dashboard answer?
(10 minutes, 5 minutes discussion)
b. What data and information should the dashboard hold? (10 minutes, 5 minutes discussion)
c. What features and functions should the dashboard have? (10 minutes, 5 minutes discussion)

Activity 2: Prioritize

After the discussion of each category, each participant receives 3 dots. They are aloud to vote for 3 of the options presented in the previous exercise.

Activity 3: 6/3/5

The 6 participants writes or sketches 3 ideas within 5 minutes on a peice of paper. After each round, the paper is passed to the left, and the next person builds on the pervious person’s idea. The prompt for this exercise was: based on our previous discussion, imagine a dashboard that can be created within 3 years.

Insight 1: Tailored systems

To answer the myriad of public health questions that analysts are tasked with, systems need to be tailored to specific questions.

Insight 2: Real-time

An effective surveillance dashboard requires that the data be updated in a timely manner.

Insight 3: Infectious disease expertise

Expert annotations and curation of infectious disease events are valuable if the tool is to be used by non-infectious disease experts.

Insight 4: Summarize when possible

Users of a surveillance dashboard for infectious diseases are not interested in all events that show up, the ability to skim is important.

Insight 5: Filter, sortable and searchable

Infectious disease events data needs to be traversed in a user-friendly manner, common functions like filter, sort, and search will be core to the experience of drilling down to relevant information.

Phase 2: Define

After the initial research exercises, we were ready to start defining the product. Based on what we discovered, I developed an elevator pitch, value proposition, and core features.

Value Proposition

1. Integration of data sources
2. Semi-synthesized information
3. Customizeable relevancy / significance

Core features

1. Ranking system (ML powered)
2. Flags + statuses
3. Centralized data (intelligently, "semi-synthesized") (ML powered)
4. Customizeable (data, time, place, feeds into ranking, flags, and data)

Data requirements

We gathered data requirements as determined by our internal subject matter experts and data science teams. These specifications determined the attributes, range of diseases, and types of aberrations that the application would accommodate.

Phase 3: Evaluate

After rounds of iteration through internal critques, we narrowed down to one direction that was suitable to conduct a stakeholder walkthrough, conduct a pilot study with a potential client, and begin gathering resources for our data science and development teams. We presented the click-through prototype with potential clients and gathered feedback through a survey sent to multiple stakeholders within the organization.

Outcomes: A first step

Delphi is not a product, it’s a prototype, the beginning of what can be a product. Its value lies in its ability to be shared with potential customers, to be tested and iterated upon. The learnings gained from this was applied to new products.
Prototyping is the conversation you have with your ideas
React.js prototype that informed BlueDot's new flagship product: Insights
Iteration of product that helped secure business partnership with Air Canada

Proof of concept with react.js

A proof of concept prototype was developed with react.js and elastic search to demostrate the ability to filter, search, and sort a feed of infectious events processed by our machine learning algorithm.

Envisioned a future to unite design, data science and development efforts

Delphi was the first articulation of a realtime surveillance system. By designing a concrete product concept, data science and development teams were able to align their efforts and begin consolidating resources to build a back-end to the application.

Building on learnings

After Delphi, BlueDot continued to iterate on the concept of a global early warning system. New products included similiar ideas explored in Delphi: real-time threat detection, tailored insights, and customized alert systems.`
